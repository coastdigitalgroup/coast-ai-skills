# Form UX Heuristics & Psychological Principles

To maximize form conversions, utilize these established principles of user
behavior and interface design.

## 1. The Sunk Cost Effect (Multi-Step Forms)

Users are more likely to complete a task if they have already invested effort
into it.

- **Application:** Start with 1-2 very easy, non-intrusive questions (e.g.,
  "What is your goal?"). Once the user clicks "Next," they have "invested" in
  the process and are significantly more likely to complete the harder fields
  (like Email or Phone) in Step 2.

## 2. The Goal-Gradient Effect

The closer a user is to a goal, the faster they work to complete it.

- **Application:** Use a progress bar in multi-step forms. Start the progress
  bar at 25% or 33% (even if they just started) to give them a sense of
  momentum.

## 3. Top-Aligned Labels (Cognitive Load)

Eye-tracking studies show that users process forms much faster when labels are
placed above the field rather than to the left.

- **Left-aligned labels** require the eye to move in a "Z" pattern (Label ->
  Field -> Label -> Field).
- **Top-aligned labels** allow the eye to move in a straight line down the page,
  reducing cognitive fatigue.

## 4. The "Email Anxiety" Principle

Users hesitate to provide email addresses due to fear of spam.

- **Mitigation:** Always place a "Trust Signal" (e.g., "We hate spam too" or
  "Privacy Protected") immediately below or next to the Email input field.

## 5. Input Masking & Auto-Formatting

Friction increases when users have to worry about formatting (e.g., dashes in
phone numbers or slashes in dates).

- **Application:** Use input masking to automatically format the data as they
  type. This reduces errors and makes the form feel "smart."

## 6. The "Submit" Button Paradox

The word "Submit" implies a one-way transfer of data where the user "gives" and
the site "takes."

- **Application:** Use "Reward-Based" language. Focus on what the user
  _receives_ (e.g., "Download Now," "Get Access," "Start My Trial").

## 7. Peak-End Rule

Users judge an experience based on how they felt at its peak and its end.

- **Application:** Make the "Success" state rewarding. Instead of a boring
  "Thank you" message, use a celebratory confirmation or immediate value (e.g.,
  "You're in! Here is your first tip...")
