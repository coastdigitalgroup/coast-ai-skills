# Pricing Psychology & Choice Architecture

Understanding how users perceive value and make decisions is critical for
optimizing a pricing page.

## Key Psychological Principles

### 1. The Decoy Effect (Asymmetric Dominance)

Users tend to have a specific change in preference between two options when also
presented with a third option that is "asymmetrically dominated."

- **Example:** A "Print Only" subscription for $60 and a "Print + Web" for $125.
  Adding a "Web Only" option for $125 (the decoy) makes the "Print + Web" option
  look like a steal.

### 2. Price Anchoring

The first price a user sees sets the "anchor" for their expectations.

- **Application:** Placing the most expensive plan on the far left can make the
  middle tier look significantly more affordable. Conversely, placing the
  "Enterprise" price clearly can make the "Pro" price look small.

### 3. The Goldilocks Effect (Center Stage Effect)

When presented with three options, humans have a natural tendency to gravitate
toward the middle one, perceiving it as the "safest" choice with the best
balance of price and features.

- **Application:** Design your "Most Popular" plan to be the middle option.

### 4. Analysis Paralysis (Hick’s Law)

The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of
choices.

- **Application:** Limit the number of plans to 3 or 4. Avoid listing every
  single minor feature in the primary pricing cards.

### 5. The Charm Pricing (Left-Digit Effect)

Prices ending in ".99" or ".95" are perceived as significantly cheaper than the
rounded-up version because we read from left to right.

- **Application:** Use $49 instead of $50, or $49.99 instead of $50.00.

### 6. Risk Aversion

Users are more motivated to avoid a loss than to achieve a gain.

- **Application:** Use language like "No-risk trial" or "Money-back guarantee"
  to lower the perceived risk of making the "wrong" choice.

## Strategic Layout Patterns

### The Tiered Card Pattern

- **Standard:** Three vertical cards.
- **Optimized:** The middle card is visually larger, has a different color
  border, or features a ribbon/badge saying "Recommended."

### The "Feature Highlight" vs. "Full Table"

- **Problem:** Long lists of features in cards create cognitive load.
- **Solution:** Show only 3-5 high-impact features (benefits) in the cards. Use
  a collapsible or separate section for the full feature comparison table.

### Price Framing

- **Framing as Daily Cost:** "Less than the price of a cup of coffee per day"
  ($3/day vs $90/mo).
- **Annual Savings:** Highlighting how much a user saves by choosing annual
  billing (e.g., "Save 20% with Annual").
