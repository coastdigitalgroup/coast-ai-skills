# PLP Optimization Heuristics & Principles

The following heuristics guide the optimization of discovery-focused pages.

## 1. The Pogo-Sticking Heuristic

**Definition:** A user clicks a product in the list, lands on the PDP, and
returns to the list within 5 seconds.

- **Insight:** This behavior signals a "failure of information" on the PLP. The
  user expected something based on the card, but the PDP immediately
  contradicted it (e.g., wrong size, unexpected price, or lack of a key
  feature).
- **Optimization:** Identify the most common reasons for the bounce and move
  those data points (size, compatibility, "ships in 24h") onto the PLP card to
  prevent the "bad click."

## 2. The 3-Filter Rule

**Definition:** Never auto-expand more than three filter categories on initial
page load.

- **Insight:** Human short-term memory and focus are limited. Showing 10
  expanded filter categories (Brand, Color, Size, Price, Material, Style, etc.)
  creates visual noise that distracts from the products.
- **Optimization:** Select the 3 facets that most strongly correlate with a
  purchase decision for that category. Hide the rest behind a "More Filters" or
  accordion toggle.

## 3. Visual Scent

**Definition:** Providing enough visual information to suggest the depth of
options available without requiring a click.

- **Insight:** If a user is looking for a "Blue Shirt" and only sees a "White
  Shirt" in the list, they may assume blue is out of stock.
- **Optimization:** Use color swatches or text labels like "+3 Colors" to
  maintain the "scent" of information and encourage clicks from users with
  specific needs.

## 4. Choice Architecture (Sorting)

**Definition:** The order of items significantly influences the "Perceived
Value" of the catalog.

- **Insight:** Users are heavily influenced by the first 4-8 items they see.
- **Optimization:**
  - **Social Proof Sort:** Defaulting to "Best Sellers" uses the "wisdom of the
    crowd" to build trust.
  - **Price Anchoring:** Occasionally placing a premium, high-priced item at the
    top can make subsequent items feel like a better value.

## 5. Mobile Interaction Cost

**Definition:** The effort required to find a product on a mobile device is
significantly higher than on desktop.

- **Insight:** Users have less "patience for pixels" on mobile. Scrolling 5
  screens to find a filter is a high interaction cost.
- **Optimization:** Use a floating "Filter" button. It's always reachable by the
  thumb, regardless of scroll position, drastically reducing the cost of
  refining the search.
