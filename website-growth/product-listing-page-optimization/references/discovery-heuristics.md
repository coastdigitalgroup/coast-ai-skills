# Discovery & Navigation Heuristics for PLPs

## 1. Interaction Cost

Interaction cost is the sum of mental and physical efforts that users must
deploy in order to reach their goals. On a PLP, the goal is to find the _right_
product.

- **High Cost:** Expanding 10 dropdowns, scrolling 5 times to find a price,
  clicking back because the size wasn't available.
- **Low Cost:** Using a "Best Seller" filter, seeing "In Stock" on the card, and
  seeing the price immediately.

## 2. The Pogo-Sticking Heuristic

In SEO, pogo-sticking is seen as a negative signal to search engines. In UX, it
is a signal of **Information Scent** failure.

- If a user clicks a product and returns within 5 seconds, the "scent" (the info
  on the card) promised something the "destination" (the PDP) didn't deliver, or
  the card failed to answer a "Dealbreaker" question (like Price or
  Compatibility).

## 3. Recognition Over Recall

Users should not have to remember what filters they applied.

- **Good:** "Filters: [Blue X] [Size: Large X] [Under $50 X]"
- **Bad:** User has to scroll back into a sidebar to see which checkboxes are
  ticked.

## 4. Hick's Law in Filtering

The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of
choices.

- By hiding low-usage filters and prioritizing the top 3 (The 3-Filter Rule),
  you reduce decision time and prevent users from feeling overwhelmed by the
  catalog.

## 5. Information Density vs. Visual Noise

- **High-Density (Good):** Showing the exact spec a user needs (e.g.,
  "Compatible with iPhone 15") on the card.
- **Visual Noise (Bad):** Adding 5 different "SALE" and "NEW" badges that
  overlap the product image.
